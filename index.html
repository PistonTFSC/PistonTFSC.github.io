Thermal-fluid-structure coupling simulation of piston in large marine diesel engine

Abstract

This paper proposes a thermal-fluid-structure coupling simulation method for pistons in large marine diesel engines. By integrating Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) to model the internal cooling cavity, we accurately identify the cooling boundary conditions, which are then mapped to the solid structure. The results demonstrate that the piston's maximum coupling stress and deformation remain within permissible limits under working conditions.

1. Geometrical Modeling

The piston is the core component of the marine diesel engine. We first established a parametric geometric model based on the key design variables such as crown length ($C_l$) and skirt length ($S_l$), as defined in Figure 1. Based on these parameters, a high-fidelity 3D model was constructed in SolidWorks (Figure 2) to capture the complex internal cooling gallery structure.

2. CFD Simulation of Cooling Cavity

2.1 Meshing & Flow Configuration

To accurately simulate the oil cooling process, we employed a multiphase flow CFD model. The irregular cooling cavity was discretized using polyhedral meshes (Figure 3), which offer superior accuracy for complex geometries. The cooling oil path, flowing from the crosshead pin through the inner pipe and spraying onto the piston crown, is illustrated in Figure 4.

Figure 3: CFD meshing of the cooling cavity using polyhedral cells.

Figure 4: Schematic of the cooling oil flow path.

2.2 Fluid Domain Results

The CFD simulation provided detailed distributions of temperature, pressure, and velocity within the cavity (Figure 5). These results serve as the precise boundary conditions for the subsequent structural analysis.

Figure 5: Cooling fluid field: (a) Temperature, (b) Pressure, and (c) Velocity distribution.

3. Solid Model & Boundary Conditions

3.1 Structural Meshing & Thermal Mapping

The solid piston was meshed using a hybrid approach of hexahedral and tetrahedral grids (Figure 6). The heat transfer coefficients and wall temperatures obtained from the CFD simulation were mapped onto this solid mesh, resulting in the non-uniform temperature distribution shown in Figure 7.

Figure 6: Structural meshing of the piston body.

Figure 7: (a) Coolant streamlines and (b) Mapped temperature distribution.

3.2 Mechanical Constraints

In addition to thermal loads, the piston is subjected to mechanical pressure from the combustion process. Figure 8 illustrates the application of the maximum explosion pressure ($P_{max} = 18.5$ MPa) on the piston crown.

Figure 8: Distribution of mechanical pressure applied to the piston.

4. Coupled Simulation Results

Finally, the thermal-fluid-structure coupling analysis was performed. Figure 9 shows the total deformation, indicating that the maximum deformation occurs at the edge of the piston crown but remains small (< 1mm). Figure 10 displays the equivalent stress distribution, identifying the top of the inner cooling bore as the region of stress concentration (419.68 MPa), which is within the material's safety limit.
