Thermal-fluid-structure coupling simulation of piston in large marine diesel engine

Abstract

This paper presents a robust thermal-fluid-structure coupling simulation framework for large marine diesel engine pistons. By integrating Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) with Finite Element Analysis (FEA), we overcome the limitations of traditional empirical formulas. Our approach explicitly models the multiphase flow within the cooling cavity to derive accurate thermal boundary conditions, ensuring precise stress and deformation predictions under extreme operating loads.

1. Geometric Modeling & Parameters

Accurate simulation begins with precise geometric representation. We established a parametric model of the piston (Figure 1), focusing on the critical dimensions of the crown, skirt, and cooling gallery. A high-fidelity 3D model (Figure 2) was constructed in SolidWorks to capture the complex internal structure, which is essential for analyzing the "shaker cooling" effect induced by piston motion.

Figure 2: Overall piston geometry (a)-(b) and sectional view (c).

2. Simulation Configuration: CFD & FEA

The coupled analysis requires high-quality discretization for both fluid and solid domains. We employed polyhedral meshes for the fluid to handle the irregular cavity shape efficiently.

Figure 3: CFD meshing of the cooling cavity. Polyhedral meshes (366,243 cells) were used to reduce computation time while maintaining accuracy.

Figure 6: Structural meshing of the solid piston. A hybrid grid of hexahedra and tetrahedra (1.4M nodes) ensures detailed stress capture.

Boundary conditions define the physics. The cooling oil circulation is modeled from the crosshead pin to the spray nozzles (Figure 4), while mechanical loads simulate the maximum explosion pressure ($P_{max}$) of 18.5 MPa (Figure 8).

Figure 4: Schematic of the cooling oil flow path.

Figure 8: Mechanical boundary conditions. The explosion pressure is applied to the piston crown with varying magnitudes.

3. Results & Discussion

3.1 Fluid Dynamics (CFD)

The multiphase flow simulation provides the foundational thermal data. We obtained distributions for temperature, pressure, and velocity within the cooling chamber.

3.2 Temperature Field

Mapping the CFD results to the solid reveals a significant temperature gradient. The highest temperature (707.63 K) occurs at the outer edge of the crown.

Figure 7: (a) Visualization of coolant flow streamlines; (b) Resulting temperature distribution on the solid piston body.

3.3 Structural Response (Deformation & Stress)

Finally, the coupled analysis combines thermal and mechanical loads. While deformation is minimal (Figure 9), stress concentration is observed near the cooling bores (Figure 10).

Figure 9: Total deformation analysis: (a) Thermal deformation dominates the crown, (b) Mechanical deformation, (c) Combined coupling deformation (Max < 1mm).

Figure 10: Equivalent Von-Mises Stress: (a) Thermal stress, (b) Mechanical stress, (c) Total Coupling stress. The peak stress (419.68 MPa) is within safety limits.
